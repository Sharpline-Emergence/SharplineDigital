Why a $500 Website Costs BC Businesses Thousands

By P. Beal — 28 years in banking & financial planning, 19 years as a small business owner, Fraser Valley, BC

Quick Read — Key Takeaways
  • A cheap website has a visible price tag — but its real costs are hidden in lost customers, poor search rankings, and platform lock-in
  • Page builder platforms charge recurring fees that add up to more than a custom build within 3–4 years
  • Slow, bloated websites built on drag-and-drop platforms consistently rank lower in Google search results
  • When you leave a page builder platform, you often can't take your website with you — you start from zero
  • A poorly designed website actively loses customers — 75% of people judge your credibility by your site alone
  • The real question isn't what a website costs to build — it's what a bad one costs you every month it's live

The pitch is hard to resist: a professional-looking website for a few hundred dollars, ready in a weekend, no technical knowledge required. Page builder platforms have made this possible for millions of small businesses — and for some, it works just fine. But for many BC small business owners, that initial saving quietly becomes one of the more expensive decisions they make. Here's an honest breakdown of what cheap websites actually cost.

01 / The Price Tag You See vs. The One You Don't

A $500 website — whether it's a template from a freelancer or a DIY build on Wix or Squarespace — has an obvious upfront cost. What it doesn't show you is the ongoing cost structure underneath it.

Most page builder platforms charge monthly or annual subscription fees that increase over time. Wix's business plans run $35–$60 per month.1 Squarespace Business sits at $36 per month.2 Add a premium theme, a booking plugin, a form tool, an email marketing integration — costs that would be built into a custom site — and you're routinely looking at $600–$1,200 per year, every year, with no end date and no asset to show for it.

A custom-coded website is a one-time build you own outright. After year one, your only ongoing cost is hosting — typically $100–$200 per year. Over a five-year period, the math is rarely as close as the upfront numbers suggest.

02 / The Hidden Cost: You Don't Own Anything

This is the part most people don't think about until it's too late. When you build a website on Wix, Squarespace, or most template-based platforms, the website lives entirely within their system. The design, the structure, the content layout — none of it is portable.

If you decide to leave the platform — because the price increases, because the company changes its terms, because you want a developer to take over — you can't take your website with you. You can export your text and images, but the site itself has to be rebuilt from scratch. Every dollar you spent on customization, every hour you invested in getting it right, is gone.

A custom-coded website is different in every respect. The files are yours. The code is yours. It can be hosted anywhere, handed to any developer, moved to any server. It's an asset in the true sense of the word — something that retains value and can be transferred.

"You can spend years building a website on someone else's platform. When you leave, you leave empty-handed."

03 / Slow Websites Lose Customers and Search Rankings

Page builder platforms generate heavy, bloated code to power their visual editing tools. Every drag-and-drop feature, every built-in animation, every theme element that you didn't use but came with the template anyway — it all adds weight to your pages. That weight slows your site down.

Google has made page speed an explicit ranking factor through its Core Web Vitals program.6 A slow site doesn't just frustrate visitors — it ranks lower in search results, meaning fewer people find you in the first place. And the visitors who do find you don't wait: 53% of mobile users abandon a page that takes more than three seconds to load.5

Custom-coded websites, built with clean HTML and CSS and hosted on modern infrastructure, load significantly faster by default. There's no platform overhead, no unused JavaScript, no template bloat. For local Fraser Valley businesses competing for the same search terms, that speed advantage translates directly into better rankings and more customers.

04 / A Bad Website Actively Loses You Business

There's a tendency to think of a cheap website as neutral — something is better than nothing, and you can upgrade later. In practice, a poorly designed or slow website isn't neutral. It works against you.

Research from Stanford's Web Credibility Project found that 75% of people judge a business's credibility based on its website design.7 A site that looks dated, loads slowly, or doesn't work properly on mobile doesn't just fail to convert visitors — it actively creates doubt about your business. For a trades contractor, a health practitioner, or a professional services firm in the Fraser Valley, that lost credibility has a direct dollar value.

Consider a conservative example: a local business that gets 200 website visitors per month. If a poor website causes even 10% of those visitors to leave without making contact — visitors who would have converted on a professional site — that's 20 lost leads per month. At an average job value of $500, that's $10,000 in lost revenue per month. The cheap website isn't saving money. It's costing it.

"The right question isn't what a website costs to build. It's what a bad one costs you every month it's live."

06 / What to Look for in a Website Investment

None of this means every small business needs to spend the maximum on a website. What it means is that the decision should be made with full information — including the costs that don't appear on the invoice.

When evaluating any website project, ask these questions: Do I own the code? Can I take it to another developer or host it anywhere? Will it load fast on mobile? Is it built to rank in search, or just to look good in a preview? What are the ongoing costs, and do they increase over time?

A well-built website is one of the few marketing investments that compounds over time — getting better search rankings, building domain authority, and working for your business around the clock without additional spend. A cheap website that needs to be rebuilt in three years isn't a saving. It's a delay.
