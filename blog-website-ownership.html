What Happens When You Stop Paying Your Web Designer: The Question Nobody Asks Until It's Too Late

By P. Beal — 28 years in banking & financial planning, 19 years as a small business owner, Fraser Valley, BC

Quick Read — Key Takeaways

  • Stop paying, and the site can go dark, get locked, or become inaccessible until the bill is settled
  • Real ownership comes down to three separate things: your domain, your hosting, and your actual code — you need control of all three, not just the one you think about
  • A lot of business owners have never actually checked whether they hold that control, because nothing has forced the question yet
  • Sharpline Digital hands over the domain, the hosting setup, and the full code on every build — cancel any time, and you keep everything

Here's a question worth asking yourself right now, before you need the answer: if you stopped paying whoever built or hosts your website tomorrow, what would happen to it? For a lot of Fraser Valley business owners, the honest answer is "I don't actually know." That's not a knock on you — it's by design. Most of the industry makes money by keeping that answer vague.

01 / The Business Model Nobody Explains Upfront

Website builders, most agencies, and a lot of freelancers all share a quiet incentive: keep you paying, indefinitely. A one-time website sale is a single transaction. A website you can never fully leave is a subscription — and subscriptions are worth more, forever, to whoever's collecting them.

That's not necessarily malicious. Platform builders like Wix and Squarespace are upfront that you're renting space on their system. But a lot of agency "maintenance plans" and freelancer retainers work the same way in practice, even when it isn't stated that plainly — the site simply doesn't function without them.

02 / Three Ways a Website Actually Gets Taken Away

This isn't hypothetical. It plays out a few common ways:

  1. The subscription lapses. Builder platforms like Wix, Squarespace, and Shopify require an active plan for the site to stay live. Miss a renewal, and the site typically goes offline, sometimes within days.
  2. The agency relationship ends. If your developer registered the domain or set up hosting under their own account — a common shortcut, especially early on — you may not have login access at all. Getting it back can mean a drawn-out request, or in worse cases, a standoff.
  3. The freelancer disappears. This happens more than people expect. A freelance developer who built your site on their own hosting account, then becomes unreachable, effectively takes your website with them — whether or not that was the intent.

"You don't find out whether you own your website until the moment you try to leave — and by then it's the wrong time to find out."

03 / What "Ownership" Actually Means Online

Ownership isn't one thing — it's three separate pieces, and missing any one of them means you don't fully control your website:

Domain
Registered in your name, in an account only you control
Hosting
The account where your site's files actually live and serve from
Code
The real files — not a black-box export locked to one platform

Most people only think about the first one. It's entirely possible to "own your domain" and still lose your working website, because the domain is just an address — it doesn't include the hosting account or the code that makes the site function.

04 / A Simple Way to Check Where You Actually Stand

Three questions, worth answering honestly right now:

  1. Can you personally log into the account where your domain is registered — not your developer's account, yours?
  2. Can you personally log into the hosting account where your site files live?
  3. If you had to move to a new developer tomorrow, could you hand them actual files, or would they be starting from a blank screen?

If any answer is "no" or "I'm not sure," that's not a crisis today — but it's worth resolving before it becomes one. Most developers and agencies will grant access if you simply ask; the problem is usually that nobody asks until the relationship is already strained.

06 / The Honest Bottom Line

Most business owners never think about this until they're already trying to switch providers, and discover the switch is harder than it should be. It doesn't have to work that way. A website is a business asset, the same as a vehicle or a piece of equipment — and like any asset, it's worth knowing whether you actually own it or you're just making payments on the right to keep using it.

If you're not sure where you stand on your current site, that's a five-minute conversation, not a project. Worth having before you need the answer, not after.
